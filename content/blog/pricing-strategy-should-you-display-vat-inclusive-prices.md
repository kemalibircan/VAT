# Pricing Strategy: Should You Display VAT Inclusive Prices?

This question comes up constantly: should your website show prices *including* VAT, or show net prices and add VAT later?

There isn’t a single right answer for every business. But there *is* a right way to keep your numbers consistent: always know whether your starting price is net or gross, and convert using the correct formula.

This is general information, not tax advice. Rates can change—confirm the correct rate for your case.

## Inclusive pricing (gross): the “what you pay” approach

VAT-inclusive pricing tends to be clearer when:

- You sell to consumers.
- You want checkout totals to match the list price.
- You want fewer customer support questions about VAT.

The trade-off: you have to reverse VAT internally to see your net revenue.

## Exclusive pricing (net): the “business maths” approach

VAT-exclusive pricing can make sense when:

- You sell B2B and customers expect net pricing.
- You do a lot of margin analysis in net terms.

The trade-off: customers might not know their final payable amount until VAT is added.

## Worked example: gross display, net planning (illustration only)

Assume you want to display a **gross** price of **60.00** and you use an **example** rate of **20%**.

1. Factor = 1 + 20/100 = 1.20
2. Net = 60.00 ÷ 1.20 = 50.00
3. VAT = 60.00 − 50.00 = 10.00

So a 60.00 gross display price implies 50.00 net revenue at the example rate.

## A simple internal rule to avoid confusion

Pick a “source of truth” price for each product:

- Either store **net** and compute gross for display, or
- Store **gross** and compute net for reporting.

Mixing both without a clear rule is where spreadsheets multiply.
