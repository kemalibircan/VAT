# VAT for Small Business: When to Use Inclusive Pricing

Small businesses often bounce between two ways of thinking:

- “What will the customer pay?” (VAT-inclusive / gross)
- “What do we earn before tax?” (VAT-exclusive / net)

This guide helps you decide which view to use in which situation, and how to convert between them using a calculator. It is general information, not tax advice. Rates can change—confirm the correct rate for your case.

## Inclusive pricing: when it’s the friendliest choice

VAT-inclusive pricing tends to work well when:

- You sell directly to consumers.
- You want fewer surprises at checkout.
- You want your marketing prices to match the final paid amount.

## Exclusive pricing: when net is clearer

VAT-exclusive pricing can be clearer when:

- You work B2B and customers expect net quotes.
- You compare supplier costs and margins in net terms.
- You need to separate tax from revenue consistently.

## Worked example: reverse VAT from a gross “budget” (illustration only)

Assume a client says: “I can spend **120.00** all-in,” and you use an **example** rate of **20%**:

1. Factor = 1 + 20/100 = 1.20
2. Net = 120.00 ÷ 1.20 = 100.00
3. VAT = 120.00 − 100.00 = 20.00

So a 120.00 gross budget implies 100.00 net and 20.00 VAT at the example rate.

## Practical tip: always label your numbers

The fastest way to avoid confusion is to label amounts as:

- “Net (ex VAT)”
- “VAT”
- “Gross (inc VAT)”

If you ever feel uncertain, run the numbers through a calculator and copy the exact breakdown.
